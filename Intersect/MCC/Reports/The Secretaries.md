## Deep Analysis: Abhik Nag (Secretary) and Terence McCutcheon (Tex)

**Elections and onboarding**

## Part 2: Terence McCutcheon (Tex)

### His formal status

Terence is introduced as an Intersect staff member who previously served as secretary. He attends meetings as a "facilitator" or support figure during the transition period. He is explicitly not an elected committee member, not the current secretary, and has no formal decision-making role.

### The scale of his participation

Terence speaks in 9 of the 11 meetings — more than most elected committee members. When he speaks, his contributions are long, detailed, and often definitional — he frames how things should be done, what tools should be used, what processes mean, what committees are for. He is one of the most influential voices in the room while holding no formal role.

### Where Terence genuinely helps

His technical knowledge of Intersect processes is real and useful. When Maureen asks about the ISC meeting structure (Dec 2), his explanation is correct and helpful. His explanation of the vice-chair transition process (Nov 18) is exactly the right framing. When Wilco asks about ClickUp blueprints (Dec 16), his honest answer that "committees are not at that level of maturity" is accurate. His point about bi-weekly meetings being more effective than weekly, based on Open Source Committee experience, is informed advice even if it was badly received.

He also holds the calendar event ownership, which is a legitimate structural role that needs resolving — and he acknowledges this needs to be transferred to Abhik.

### Where Terence goes beyond facilitation

**1. Blocking committee decisions he disagrees with**

The most striking example is Dec 16. When Maureen asks for direct access to the MCC X account, Lorenzo says it's a formal Intersect account requiring oversight. Terence goes further, delivering a speech about how other committees "fight for access and then don't do anything." This isn't neutral facilitation — he is invoking institutional history to persuade the committee to accept a restriction they're unhappy with.

When Maureen pushes back on the ClickUp setup in Dec 16, Terence says: "I'm hearing intersect staff level requests from what the committee wants to do with no foundational backing." This is a direct criticism of the committee's legitimacy, delivered by someone who has no formal authority over them. He then calls for the committee to decide whether his support is wanted — which sounds democratic but is actually a pressure tactic: it forces the committee to either publicly rebuff him (which feels hostile) or implicitly accept his authority.

**2. Controlling meeting structure**

In Dec 2, Terence repeatedly redirects the agenda. He interrupts the Nathan replacement discussion to say essentially "you could spend a month on this or just move on." In Dec 9, he tells the committee that the lead of the Office Hours initiative should decide the time unilaterally — removing the committee's collective decision from the table. In Dec 16, he tells Maureen to move check-ins to a different meeting. In Jan 20, he delivers a long speech defending the bi-weekly meeting decision as if it were his decision to defend.

These are a former secretary's instincts running on autopilot: he sees inefficiency and reaches for the steering wheel even though he's no longer the driver.

**3. Framing what MCC is allowed to want**

In Dec 16, he says: "I'm hearing intersect staff level requests from what the committee wants to do." This reframe — "you're asking for things above your level" — is a significant power move. The committee then has to argue for their own legitimacy to ask for basic operational tools.

In Feb 3, when Maureen and Wilco ask whether elected members can have internal calls and create their own chats, Terence and Abhik give inconsistent answers ("you're always welcome to meet on your own" from Abhik, but earlier "the work needs to be in the official channels" from Terence's general posture). The result is genuine confusion — as Maureen says in Feb 3: "So is it no longer illegal to organize calls by ourselves and create chats?" Wilco says "No, we were shut down." This confusion was created in significant part by Terence's earlier framing.

**4. The Dec 16 confrontation — and Maureen's direct response**

The most revealing moment in the series. After a heated ClickUp access dispute, Terence issued a binary ultimatum framed as a democratic choice:

> *"Would you like my continued support as a facilitator, having successfully led other committees, and I can walk you through each and every step of this process... or if I'm not helping and continuing to cause what seems to be a disturbance in this meeting, then I will simply fully step away and allow Abhik to take it over here."*

This is a pressure tactic, not a genuine offer. It forced the committee to either publicly rebuff him (uncomfortable) or implicitly endorse his authority. But Maureen did not accept the framing. Her response was direct and unambiguous:

> *"I want your support to just hand over everything you have to Abhik so that he can do it work properly. That's the support I need from you right now. Right now he cannot write meeting notes because you have all the access to do that. You haven't handed it to him I don't know how long. So I want meeting notes in 48 hours."*

This is not "yes, please stay and keep facilitating." This is: hand over your access, step out of the operational role, and let Abhik do his job. Maureen is telling Terence to exit the day-to-day and remove the structural bottleneck he represents. There was no vote, no committee consensus to keep Terence in a facilitation role — the meeting moved on without a formal resolution, and Terence continued attending. The absence of a formal ejection should not be read as endorsement.

Earlier in the same meeting, Terence had also said to the committee:

> *"I've gone through this conversation, this exact conversation now with a number of different bodies... what happens is is everyone fights and says, 'Oh, well, I just need the access. I just need the access.' and then they don't do anything because they didn't get what they wanted."*

This is a visiting staff member telling an elected committee that their frustrations are a predictable pattern of failure he has seen before — and therefore dismissible. Whether accurate or not, this is not the language of a neutral facilitator. He is using institutional memory to delegitimize the committee's concerns before they have been addressed.

**5. Advocating for specific governance structures**

Terence consistently advocates for bi-weekly meetings (Dec 2, Dec 16, Jan 20) as if this is a neutral best practice, but he does so with reference to "success in the open source committee" — which is his own past work. He is using his OSC experience as a template and attempting to impose it on MCC, which has different membership, different challenges, and a Chair who explicitly wants different things.

In Mar 17, when the handbook discussion comes up, Terence says: "the charter is essentially the handbook for how the committee operates" — and then proceeds to share his screen and show the OSC charter as the model. He is steering the committee toward his preferred structure.

**6. Validating or invalidating committee outputs**

This is Terence reporting off-record conversations to the whole committee as a way of introducing an external judgment about the committee's functioning. He then says "deadlines are absolutely flexible at this point" — a statement that directly contradicts Maureen's urgency and the budget timeline Lorenzo described. He is not facilitating; he is countermanding.

### The deeper dynamic

What the transcripts reveal is a structural power asymmetry that neither man is fully acknowledging. Terence holds the calendar event ownership (and therefore the auto-generated transcripts), has institutional history with the committee, has relationships with senior Intersect staff, and knows the tools better than anyone in the room. Abhik defers to him repeatedly. The elected Chair is frequently in a position of arguing for things that Terence or Abhik are pushing back on — and she rarely wins those arguments cleanly.

The Dec 16 exchange is the clearest illustration: Maureen explicitly told Terence to hand everything over to Abhik and get out of the operational role. That instruction was not followed. Terence continued to attend and influence meetings for the remaining three months of the period covered. The Chair's direct request had no effect — which itself reveals the nature of the power asymmetry.

Terence is not malicious. He clearly believes he is helping. But "helping from a position of superior knowledge and no formal accountability" is a specific kind of interference. He can say almost anything — that the committee's requests are unfounded, that their frustration is predictable, that their preferred meeting cadence is wrong — with no electoral consequences. An elected committee member who overreaches can be voted out. Terence cannot.

His suggestion about the Miro board as a collaborative space for non-committee members was practical and adopted. ### Overall verdict on Terence

He operates well beyond the scope of a neutral facilitator. In meetings he functions more like a shadow governance head — setting the terms of debate, controlling tool access via calendar ownership, delivering long speeches that reframe the committee's agency, and using institutional history to preempt criticism. The most telling moment: the elected Chair directly instructed him to hand over access and step out of the operational role. He did not comply. The fact that this is largely done with good intentions doesn't change the effect. An elected committee should be able to make its own mistakes and develop its own processes. Terence's presence, well-intentioned as it is, consistently compressed the space for that to happen.

## Combined Dynamic

The two together create a specific problem: Abhik defers to Terence on major decisions ("text you want to add anything?"), which means an unelected staff member effectively has veto power through a two-step relay. The elected Chair is left arguing with both of them simultaneously, often on basic operational matters like who can attend a meeting or whether a vote happened correctly. The summaries — Abhik's formal output — then present this contested space as smooth consensus, completing the loop.

The committee deserves a secretary who documents faithfully, prepares agendas with the Chair, ensures tools work, and stays off the governance stage. It also deserves a facilitator — if one is needed at all — whose role is explicitly bounded and time-limited, not indefinitely present with full speaking rights and institutional memory as leverage.
