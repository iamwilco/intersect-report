Report 03

The Secretaries

A critical examination of two non-elected staff figures whose influence on the MCC significantly exceeded their formal roles: Secretary Abhik Nag, and former Secretary and Intersect staff member Terence McCutcheon (Tex). Both are assessed against the defined Secretary role responsibilities.

Secretary role — defined responsibilities

  • Maintain committee documentation and minutes
  • Prepare agendas with Chair and Vice Chair
  • Manage communication channels per Intersect policies
  • Manage committee calendar and operational tools
  • Maintain the committee delivery plan
  • Support committee elections and new member onboarding
  • Ensure Code of Conduct and policy compliance
  • Ensure sufficient quorum for votes
  • Ensure compliance with Robert's Rules during votes
  • Maintain meeting pace to agenda

What the role explicitly excludes

  • Leading discussion or setting direction
  • Taking or advocating positions on governance decisions
  • Influencing votes or redirecting vote outcomes
  • Shaping committee strategy or workplan
  • Inviting guests without committee sanction
  • Co-authoring proposals the committee will vote on
  • Asserting authority over what the committee is allowed to want
  • Using institutional memory to pre-empt committee autonomy

Part 1: Abhik Nag — Secretary

Meeting minutes and documentation

This is the most visible failure. Meeting minutes were chronically late throughout the series. In Nov 25, Laurentine explicitly says she never receives minutes. Abhik apologises, citing the Cardano Summit. In Dec 16, Maureen asks for a 48-hour turnaround window. Abhik then reveals he doesn't even receive the auto-generated transcript — because Terence owns the calendar event and gets the notes automatically. He is structurally blocked from his own primary responsibility, and the handover is still incomplete by the series' end.

When he does produce summaries, the quality analysis in Report 01 found they contain fabricated action items, invented decisions, and systematically sanitised conflict. This is not a neutral documentation failure — it actively distorts the institutional record.

Agenda preparation with the Chair

There is no evidence across 11 transcripts that Abhik prepared a single meeting agenda collaboratively with Maureen. In Nov 18 Darlington searches for an agenda while Abhik checks Discord. In Dec 16 Maureen says: "I wrote the agenda today on Google Docs because I was running late. I had five minutes to write it and ClickUp is a b***h." The Chair is writing her own agendas in a last-minute scramble. Abhik's involvement in agenda preparation is absent from the transcripts.

Managing committee tools and delivery plan

This is one of the most persistent sources of dysfunction in the entire series. Committee members repeatedly cannot create tasks, cannot create forms, cannot create dashboards. Abhik's response oscillates between offering 101 training sessions and saying he'll speak to Terence. By Feb 3, Fanny says she can't add tasks, Maureen says "let's just forget about ClickUp," and Wilco says "no one is doing anything about it." 101 sessions are promised across multiple meetings (Dec 16, Jan 20, Feb 3) and are either never scheduled or ineffective — by Mar 3 the ClickUp board remains a problem.

Communication channel management

He runs the X account posting queue via Buffer (within scope) and coordinates the Dev Report inclusion of MCC updates (within scope). But he also posts the X Space link without advance notice (criticised in Jan 6), fails to post Chaya's original content for weeks despite it being in the shared spreadsheet (Wilco flags this in Jan 20), and receives Chaya's content emails at a wrong address for an extended period (Dec 16).

Where Abhik crosses his lane

1. Actively swaying votes and procedural decisions

In Feb 17, Maureen is holding a live vote on whether non-committee members can attend the Thursday budget session. Abhik intervenes repeatedly: "I think best would be to come on Discord. I know everyone's on Discord. we'll make a poll there." When Maureen pushes back, Abhik says "I'm just trying to make the time useful" and continues lobbying for the Discord poll even as Maureen is counting votes. This is not maintaining quorum — this is actively redirecting the outcome of a vote.

2. Making strategic recommendations

In Jan 6: "I highly recommend every elected member to have a call on your own to make sure you guys are aligned." In Jan 20, he argues the case for bi-weekly meetings. In Feb 3: "I will again reemphasize on this. I highly recommend every elected member to have a meeting every week discussing on these remnants." Recommending governance structure and meeting strategy is not the secretary's role.

3. Bringing unapproved guests to meetings

Abhik brought DoraHacks (Steve) to Feb 3 and Mar 3 on his own initiative. He orchestrated the Grid presentation (Jan 20) by coordinating with Jeremy Firster independently. He brought Meek Owen to Jan 6. None of these guest invitations are documented as committee decisions. He is using his administrative access to shape what the committee hears, which is a meaningful form of agenda control.

4. Influencing the hub proposal process

In Feb 3, Akheel says: "ABS is also helping us with regards to how to plan the process, how to do certain things for the proposal." The secretary is co-authoring the committee's most important deliverable — the hub proposal — which is governance work, not administration. In Mar 3, he actively defends and explains the DoraHacks/C# hackathon proposal, lobbying for a specific budget line item to be included.

5. The summaries as institutional control

As analysed in Report 01, the summaries systematically: convert proposals into decisions, invent action items that were never agreed, remove conflict and frustration, and attribute smooth consensus to messy situations. A secretary who writes inaccurate minutes holds significant power over institutional memory — regardless of intent, this shapes how events are understood by anyone who reads the official record.

Verdict — Abhik Nag

Performs some secretary functions adequately — attendance, basic communications — but consistently fails at the most critical ones: timely minutes, agenda preparation, ClickUp access. More importantly, he systematically overreaches into governance: advocating positions, influencing votes, shaping the agenda through unsanctioned guest invitations, co-authoring proposals, and producing summaries that distort the record. The role requires a neutral facilitator. What the committee received was a participant with administrative access.

Part 2: Terence McCutcheon (Tex)

Formal status

Terence is an Intersect staff member who previously served as MCC Secretary. He attends meetings as a "facilitator" or support figure during the transition. He is explicitly not an elected committee member, not the current secretary, and has no formal decision-making role.

He speaks in 9 of the 11 meetings — more than most elected committee members. When he speaks, contributions are long, detailed, and often definitional: he frames how things should be done, what tools should be used, what processes mean, what committees are for. He is one of the most influential voices in the room while holding no formal role.

Where Terence genuinely helps

His institutional knowledge is real and valuable when used to answer specific questions. His explanation of the vice-chair transition process (Nov 18) is exactly right. His honest admission that "committees are not at a maturity level to blueprint-share" (Dec 16) is accurate and saves effort. His suggestion of the Miro board as a collaborative space for non-committee members was practical and adopted. He holds the calendar event — a legitimate structural role that needs resolution, which he acknowledges.

Blocking committee decisions he disagrees with

In Dec 16, Maureen asks for direct access to the MCC X account. Terence goes beyond policy explanation, delivering a speech about how other committees "fight for access and then don't do anything." This is invoking institutional history to persuade the committee to accept a restriction they oppose. When Maureen pushes back on the ClickUp setup, Terence says: "I'm hearing intersect staff level requests from what the committee wants to do with no foundational backing." This directly criticises the committee's legitimacy — from someone with no formal authority over them.

The Dec 16 confrontation — and what Maureen actually said

After the ClickUp dispute escalated, Terence issued a binary ultimatum framed as a democratic choice:

"Would you like my continued support as a facilitator, having successfully led other committees, and I can walk you through each and every step of this process... or if I'm not helping and continuing to cause what seems to be a disturbance in this meeting, then I will simply fully step away and allow Abhik to take it over here."

This is a pressure tactic, not a genuine offer. It forces the committee to either publicly rebuff Terence (uncomfortable) or implicitly endorse his continued authority. But Maureen did not accept either option on his terms. Her response was direct and unambiguous:

"I want your support to just hand over everything you have to Abhik so that he can do it work properly. That's the support I need from you right now. Right now he cannot write meeting notes because you have all the access to do that. You haven't handed it to him I don't know how long. So I want meeting notes in 48 hours."

This is not "yes, please stay and keep facilitating." This is the Chair telling Terence to hand over his access, step out of the operational role, and let the actual Secretary do his job. No vote was held. No committee consensus formed to retain Terence as a facilitator. The meeting moved on without formal resolution — and Terence continued attending for the remaining three months of the period. The Chair's direct instruction had no effect.

Controlling meeting structure

In Dec 2, Terence interrupts the Nathan replacement discussion to say essentially "you could spend a month on this or just move on." In Dec 9, he tells the committee that the Office Hours lead should decide the timing unilaterally — removing the committee's collective decision. In Dec 16, he tells Maureen to move check-ins to a different meeting. In Jan 20, he delivers a long speech defending the bi-weekly meeting decision as if it were his to defend.

Framing what MCC is allowed to want

His repeated framing — "you're asking for things above your level," "the OSC does it this way," "bi-weekly is proven to work" — creates a situation where the committee has to argue for its own legitimate preferences against someone with no electoral accountability. An elected committee member who overreaches can be voted out. Terence cannot.

In Mar 3

"I was approached last week by multiple individuals, committee and non-committee members about basically how collaboration is working or rather not working right now." This introduces off-record conversations as a way of validating an external judgment about the committee's functioning — without naming sources or allowing the committee to verify or respond. He then says "deadlines are absolutely flexible at this point" — directly contradicting Maureen's urgency and the timeline Lorenzo had described.

Verdict — Terence McCutcheon

Operates well beyond the scope of a neutral facilitator. The clearest evidence: in Dec 16, the elected Chair directly instructed him to hand over all access to Abhik and step out of the operational role. He did not comply, and continued attending and influencing meetings for the remaining three months. Beyond that incident, he functions as a shadow governance head — setting the terms of debate, holding calendar access as structural leverage, delivering speeches that reframe the committee's agency, and using institutional history to preempt criticism. An elected committee should be able to make its own mistakes and develop its own processes. Terence's presence consistently compressed the space for that to happen.

The Combined Dynamic

The two together create a specific and serious problem. Abhik defers to Terence on major decisions — "text you want to add anything?" — which means an unelected staff member effectively has veto power through a two-step relay. The elected Chair is left arguing with both simultaneously, often on basic operational matters like who can attend a meeting or whether a vote happened correctly.

Terence holds the calendar event ownership (and therefore the auto-generated transcripts), has institutional history with the committee, has relationships with senior Intersect staff, and knows the tools better than anyone in the room. The summaries — Abhik's formal output — then present this contested space as smooth consensus, completing the loop.

The committee deserves a secretary who documents faithfully, prepares agendas with the Chair, ensures tools work, and stays off the governance stage. It also deserves a facilitator — if one is needed at all — whose role is explicitly bounded and time-limited, not indefinitely present with full speaking rights and institutional memory as leverage.
